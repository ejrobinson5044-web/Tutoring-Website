      Developmental grouping

      Age paths for EF instruction

      The bands below keep early learning caregiver-led, then gradually shift toward learner-owned systems as planning, monitoring, and independence become realistic goals.

      What gets taught

      Three core EF muscles, one visible routine

      Each age path practices working memory, inhibitory control, and cognitive flexibility through a repeated cycle: name the demand, choose a tool, use it in a real task, then reflect.

      Working Memory

      Holding instructions, goals, and steps in mind long enough to use them.

      Tools: visual plans, rehearsal, chunking, checklist starts.

      Inhibitory Control

      Pausing, resisting distractions, waiting, and matching action to purpose.

      Tools: stop-go games, timers, cues, pre-commitment prompts.

      Cognitive Flexibility

      Switching strategies, seeing another viewpoint, and adjusting after errors.

      Tools: rule-switching, plan B mapping, compare-and-revise.

      Why these bands

      Literature-informed, classroom-practical

      Harvard's EF activity guide uses age sections from infancy through adolescence. This site preserves those developmental shifts, combines 6-18 and 18-36 months as caregiver-led foundations, and splits later childhood and adolescence where instruction changes sharply.

      01

      Early years need adult scaffolding

      Infants and toddlers practice EF through imitation, turn-taking, language, songs, movement, and predictable routines.

      02

      School-age learners need tools

      Ages 5-12 move from rule games and play toward checklists, planning, self-monitoring, and strategy talk tied to actual school demands.

      03

      Adolescence deserves finer cuts

      Large-sample work reports rapid EF development around 10-15, with maturation continuing toward 18-20, so teen lessons should become progressively more learner-owned.
